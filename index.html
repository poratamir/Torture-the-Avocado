🥑 Torture the Avocado

🌱 How growth works

The stem is a 3D curve. Growth occurs along the green growth zone — when one side elongates faster than the other, the stem bends. The brown mature zone is permanently fixed.

Slow the growth rate right down to watch curvature build step by step.

🪴 Torturing the plant

The classic way to torture a houseplant is to rotate the pot — the stem was growing toward the window, and now gravity and light pull it a different way. Use the Pot tab to tilt and spin. You can also move the light source to the other side and watch the plant chase it. What shape can you grow?

Rotate the pot 90°, then add a light on the opposite side — gravity and light will pull the stem in competing directions.

⬆ Gravitropism

Plants sense gravity and grow against it (shoots) or toward it (roots). Negative sensitivity = shoot-like, grows upward. Positive = root-like, grows downward.

Rotate the pot 90° and watch the plant slowly correct itself.

☀ Phototropism

Add a point light source and the plant bends toward it. Sensitivity follows Stevens' power law — response grows non-linearly with signal strength. Drag the ☀ sphere to reposition it; use the height slider to move it up and down.

Try slowly rotating the light around the plant and watch the stem follow.

🔄 Autotropism (proprioception)

The plant senses its own curvature and resists over-bending — like an internal straightening drive. Without it the stem would spiral without limit. The balance number B = β·L_gz / γ describes whether autotropism dominates (B≪1, smooth straightening) or the tropism response overshoots (B≫1, oscillatory tip).

Set γ = 0 and watch the stem curl out of control.

🌀 Circumnutations

Many plant shoots spontaneously spiral their growing tip in circles — driven by an internal oscillator, not any external signal. This helps climbing plants search for supports. You can tune the amplitude and period.

Combine circumnutations with a light source for rich 3D paths.

Δ Differential growth vectors

The Δ tab overlays arrows showing the differential growth direction at each cross-section of the growth zone — the direction in which one side of the stem elongates faster than the other. Each arrow is the vector sum of all active stimuli (gravity, light, autotropism, circumnutations) projected onto the stem cross-section. Arrow length is proportional to magnitude.
